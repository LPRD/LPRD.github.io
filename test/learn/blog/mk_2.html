Time to get Technical: Mk. 2 Engine Film Coolant Injector

The Mk. 2 Engine is a second-generation bipropellant rocket engine designed by the LPRD1 Rocketry team at the University of Minnesota, Twin Cities. With a design thrust of 225 lbf, this engine will be flight capable. The engine is designed to run on kerosene fuel, with gaseous oxygen acting as the oxidizer, and a nominal chamber pressure of 300 psi. There are three versions of the Mk. 2 system: an all-aluminum water-cooled version for ground testing, an additively manufactured Inconel combustion chamber with regenerative cooling tubes, donated by ProtoLabs, and an ablative version. The purpose of the Mk. 2 program is to bridge the gap between the instructional Mk. 1 engine and the large high-performance Mk. 3 engine, being designed for the heroX Base 11 Space Challenge. The Mk. 2 testing program will evaluate two forms of engine thermal control: active control using regenerative and film cooling, and passive control, using ablative materials. This document will focus on the film-cooled portion of the active control design.

Active Cooling System

The original Mk. 2 concept was to use entirely regenerative cooling, resulting in the fabrication of the additively manufactured Inconel combustion chamber, whose specifications are given in Appendix A. However, after full analysis was conducted it was determined that regenerative cooling alone would be inadequate, due to the small scale of the engine leading to a disparity between heated surface area and fuel flow rate. The alternative to ordering constructionof a completely new engine was to redesign the injector to provide film coolant, the process of which is detailed below.

Design Process

Following analysis, it was determined that the regenerative system would only be able to remove about 20% of the heat energy given off by the combustion. Therefore, it was decided that the film coolant system would be designed to entirely cool the engine, allowing the regenerative system to serve as a form of redundancy. The mass flow rate of kerosene coolant was determined using the formula given in Appendix B. There was often uncertainty about the exact value of some of the parameters, and in all cases the most conservative option was chosen. The exact values used are presented in Table 1 (Appendix B).The MATLAB script used to evaluate the equation is presented in Appendix C. The calculated flow rates were equal to approximately fifty percent of the combusted fuel flow rate, resulting in an injector geometry of eight one-millimeter orifices. However, to achieve uniform wall coverage a diverging flat spray pattern is required. Machining a slit at thissmall size was determined to be impractical, so an alternative method was chosen, using commercial flat spray nozzles impinging on the wall at an angle of thirteen degrees.

Fabrication

Aluminum stock of five-inchdiameter was cut to length on a chop saw, then faced with a lathe. The upstream side was then turned on a lathe, to create the oxygen stem. The lathe was also used to finish the downstream face. The injector was then mounted on an adjustable turntable, and a mill was used to create the bolt holes, oxygen stem, O-ring groove, impinging injector, and spray nozzle adaptors. An NPT drill tap attached to the mill was used to thread the oxygen stem and spray nozzle adaptors.

Testing Plan

The film coolant system will be hot fire tested using the aluminum water jacket, the full assembly is shown in Figure 1.

Figure 1. This figure shows the Mk. 2 engine assembly, with arrows denoting key locations.

The water coolant jacket will be used to insure safety and to preserve the test article if the film coolant system fails. Thermocouples will be placed in the locations shown, to gather temperature data at the throat. A series of three ten-second burns will be used to determine the effectiveness of the film coolant and its impact on the performance of the engine. Testing will be conducted at Northrop Grumman Innovation Systems Proving Ground in Elk River Minnesota.
